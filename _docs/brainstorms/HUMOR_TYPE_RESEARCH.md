## 0. How to use this document (read first)

**No real jokes exist in this game.** All comedic content is performed in
videogame gibberish — non-language vocalization (think Simlish, Animal
Crossing's "Animalese," Undertale's bark beeps), paired with animation,
action, and sound design. There is no joke-writing task here, ever.

**So why study real comedy theory?** Because the *structure* of a joke —
timing, setup/payoff, audience expectation, rhythm — is what you're encoding
into **performance signatures**, not the *content*. Aristotle's incongruity,
Bergson's rigidity, and the setup→punchline anatomy below all describe
*shapes* a comedic beat can take. Your job is to translate each shape into:

| Comedy-theory concept | Translates to (performance) |
|---|---|
| Setup → punchline | A held beat (animation pause) → a sharp release (pose + VFX pop) |
| Timing / the pause | Literal delay between vocal cadence syllables before the "hit" |
| Misdirection | A cadence that implies one direction (rising pitch) then resolves elsewhere |
| Callback | A repeated cadence/action motif reused later in a fight, recontextualized |
| Audience read (who laughs at what) | Which performance signature (cadence/action) scores Strong/Weak vs. which temperament |

Read everything below with that translation table in mind. If you catch
yourself drafting an actual punchline or sample line, stop — convert it to a
cadence/timing/action description instead.

## 1. Comedy structures & typologies (brief survey)

### Aristotle / classical comedy theory
- **Comedy is recognition of superiority** — the audience laughs at the
  protagonist's *flaw* or *fall* (the tragic hero inverted).
- Core affects: **relief, superiority, incongruity**. A laugh is a recognition
  that something is *wrong in a safe way*.

### Modern / applied typologies (used in comedy writing)

**Bergson (1900):** Comedy arises from **rigidity** — a character who is
*mechanical*, repetitive, unable to adapt. The laugh is society punishing
inflexibility. (*Slapstick* = the body as a machine; *dry wit* = the mind
locked in pattern.)

**Bremmer & Roodenburg (1997):** comedy spans:
- **Physical** (slapstick, visual gags, prop comedy)
- **Verbal** (wordplay, puns, one-liners, observational)
- **Situational** (timing, context, incongruity, dark irony)
- **Character** (farce, misidentification, role-reversal, pathos)

**Stand-up anatomy (modern):**
- **Setup → Punchline** — the frame + the twist.
- **Callbacks** — returning to an earlier joke with new context.
- **Misdirection** — the audience expects X, you deliver Y.
- **Timing** — the gap between setup and payoff is comedic tension.

**Audience temperament (who laughs at what):**
- *Highbrow / sophisticated* → wordplay, meta-commentary, intellectual irony.
- *Sentimental / emotional* → self-deprecation, pathos, recognition of shared
  struggle ("sad clown").
- *Physical / rowdy* → slapstick, pratfalls, absurdist chaos.
- *Anarchic / transgressive* → shock, dark humor, subversion of taboo.
- *Observational / "regular folk"* → everyday relatability, slight exaggeration.

### The read (genre-awareness in comedy)
The best comedians **read the room** — they sense the audience's mood,
sophistication, energy, and **shift their material accordingly**. A dark joke
to a tense crowd can diffuse; to a conservative crowd, it bombs. Same joke,
different temperament, opposite effect.

## 2. MTG color wheel as a metaphor for humor design

Magic: the Gathering organizes ~240 keywords across 5 colors in a wheel:
- **Each color has core identity** (Blue = control/knowledge, Red = chaos/action,
  Green = nature/growth, White = order/community, Black = ambition/power).
- **Adjacent colors (splash zones) share keywords** (Blue-Green shares both
  "draw cards" and "growth" themes).
- **Opposite colors oppose mechanically** (Blue vs. Red, Green vs. White, etc.).
- **Every card is authored to a color** — no "colorless" cards that do everything.

Why this works as a humor model:
- **Distinct identity:** "I want a joke that lands on the pompous intellectual"
  → reach for high-brow, not low-brow.
- **Meaningful opposition:** some humor types actively *fail* against certain
  temperaments (shock doesn't land on the sensitive; slapstick bombs on the snob).
- **Splash zones (adjacent types):** wordplay and observational humor both
  appeal to thoughtful audiences, but serve different aspects of how they think.
- **Authored, not generated:** no infinite joke-generator; a fixed, curated set
  of "parent types" with sub-specializations.

**The key:** the wheel is a *scarcity model*. Not every joke is good for every
crowd. The read is the *expertise* in picking the right joke for the room.

## 3. Candidate humor parent types (seeds — not locked)

These are starting points drawn from structure theory and stand-up tradition.
**Your job is to choose, name, and define the actual 3–5 parents for Clowns.**

| Parent type | Core mechanism | Audience it lands on | Audience it bombs on | Example specialization |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Slapstick / Physical** | Body as machine; repetition; pratfalls | Rowdy, physical, children, uninhibited crowds | Sophisticated, cerebral | Exaggerated falls, prop mishandling, escalating chaos |
| **High-brow / Intellectual** | Wordplay, meta-commentary, cultural references | Educated, literary, snobby, theater crowds | Rowdy, working-class, "didn't get it" | Puns, literary allusion, deconstruction, subtext |
| **Dark / Transgressive** | Taboo, shock, subversion of norms | Anarchic, rebellious, adults wanting permission | Sensitive, conservative, rule-bound | Death jokes, body horror, profanity, irony |
| **Self-deprecating / Pathos** | The clown's failure; relatability; shared struggle | Sentimental, empathetic, emotionally available | Detached, competitive, "weakness is pathetic" | Admissions of loss, physical/social inadequacy, vulnerability |
| **Observational / Relatable** | "Why is X like this?" recognizing the mundane | Thoughtful, everyday-folk, self-aware | Avant-garde, "too highbrow to relate," detached snobs | Exaggerated truths about dating, work, food, modern life |
| **Absurdist / Chaotic** | No rules; random juxtaposition; RNG | Anarchic, surrealist, comedy nerds, Gen-Z irony | Coherence-lovers, literal-minded, context-dependent | Non-sequiturs, stream-of-consciousness, the rule is there is no rule |
| **Callbacks / Narrative** | Rewarding the attentive; building context | Invested audiences, repeat viewers | One-off encounters, unfamiliar crowds | Returning to a setup with new layers |

**Reading the "Example specialization" column:** these are conceptual labels
(e.g., "puns," "literary allusion") describing the *category* of comedic
mechanism — not text to be written or spoken. Per §0, translate each into a
cadence/animation/action signature (e.g., "puns" → a cadence with a
deliberate mid-phrase pitch-twist, as if two meanings are colliding, no actual
wordplay text required).

## 4. Audience temperament axis (seeds — not locked)

Your enemies/hecklers represent crowd types. Temperament is what the player
*reads* to decide which humor to reach for.

| Temperament | Laughs most at | Resistant to | Readable tell | Example crowd archetype |
|---|---|---|---|---|
| **Pompous / Intellectual** | Wit, references, competence | Slapstick, shock, anything "low" | Formal dress, upright posture, nods | Theatre snob, academic, socialite |
| **Rowdy / Rowdy** | Slapstick, shock, anarchic | Subtle wordplay, patience, reflection | Loud laughter, movement, energy | Sports bar patrons, rowdy bachelor party |
| **Sensitive / Emotional** | Self-deprecation, pathos, relatability | Shock, dark humor, cruelty | Nodding at emotional beats, visible emotion | People tearing up at stories |
| **Observational / Everyday** | Recognizable truths, relatability | Obscure reference, total chaos | Genuine laughter at familiarity | Regular folks, "I relate" energy |
| **Anarchic / Detached** | Absurdist, chaotic, subversive | Sentimentality, earnest emotion | Smirking, knowing nods | Irony-posture crowds, nihilists |

## 5. The read as core mechanic (MTG parallel)

In MTG, **building a deck** is about choosing a color identity and committing to
it — a Blue-Green deck does different things than a Red-White deck, and
knowing which to build is *deck design skill*. You don't get to do everything.

In Clowns, **reading the crowd** is the player skill. The temperament is
*visible*; the player's job is to identify it and reach into their humor
toolkit for the type that matches. Doing this well is the read; doing it wrong
is the bomb.

**The design leverage:** if you author the wheel so that:
1. Each parent type has a **clear identity** (Pillar 2 requires distinctness).
2. Each temperament is **readable at a glance** (Pillar 3 requires feedback).
3. No single parent is **strong against everything** (Pillar 2 forbids it).
4. The **splash zones** (adjacent parents) add texture without erasing the
   identity.

...then the read is *a real decision*, and the toy works.

## 6. Practical design questions for your wheel

Answer these as you build:

1. **How many parents?** Recommend **3–5**. Below 3: no real read (all jokes are
   similar). Above 5: content explosion + the temperament axis can't differentiate.
2. **Symmetry?** Should the wheel be **balanced** (each parent roughly equal
   power) or **swingy** (some types are high-risk/high-reward)? Swingy is more
   fun for reads; balanced is safer.
3. **Who's adjacent to whom?** Draw the wheel. State adjacencies explicitly.
   Example: Slapstick is adjacent to Absurdist (both chaotic); both oppose
   High-brow (cerebral).
4. **Per-parent specializations?** Each parent has sub-types. Slapstick might
   split into "exaggerated pratfalls" vs. "prop misuse." Keep it small and
   authored (no rules-editor surface).
5. **Readability of temperament?** Can a player tell at a glance? Is there a
   visual tell, a sound cue, an animation? (Pillar 3.)
6. **Readability of matchup?** When the player chooses wrong, does it *look
   wrong* (meter doesn't spike, enemy flinches away)? Can they learn?

## 7. References & further reading (optional deep dives)

- **Bergson, *Laughter* (1900):** the theory of rigidity and the mechanical.
- **Bremmer & Roodenburg, *A Cultural History of Humour* (1997):** taxonomy of
  comedy types.
- **Stand-up comedy craft:** watch comedians with different styles and note how
  they *build* a joke (setup, misdirection, punchline, callback).
- **Magic: the Gathering color identity:** study how colors are differentiated
  and how splice zones work. The wheel's *constraints* are what make it work.
- **Theatre / clown traditions:** Pierrot (sad clown), Auguste (anarchic clown),
  tramp clown (everyman). Each has a traditional comedic voice — good model for
  class/temperament pairing.

## 8. Design red flags (avoid these)

- **Every type is strong against one temperament and weak against one.** This
  creates a static, *solved* read (Rock-Paper-Scissors). Add at least one
  temperament where the type is neutral, or one where it's nuanced.
- **A type with no weakness.** Violates Pillar 2 (Read the Room). If it always
  lands, there is no read.
- **More than ~8 types at MVP.** Content explosion; the player can't learn the
  wheel.
- **Sub-types are *generated* by rules.** (e.g., "combine any two parents =
  hybrid type"). Stay authored and finite (cut-list #4).
- **Temperament is invisible.** If the crowd doesn't *signal* its temperament,
  the player is guessing. Readability is Pillar 3.
- **Mechanics divorced from flavor.** ("Slapstick does high burst" makes sense;
  "Slapstick gives you 15% haste" does not.) Every mechanic should trace to the
  type's core identity.
- **Any real, written punchline, sample joke, or comedic text sneaking into the
  design doc "as an example."** Even as a placeholder, this leaks the wrong
  mental model. Describe cadence/timing/action instead — always (see §0).
